**Training Module for Documenting Threats or Harm**.  
This module is designed for **protective‑intelligence teams, security staff, HR, supervisors, and any personnel who may need to document threatening or harmful behavior**.

It is structured like a **professional training curriculum**, with:

- Learning objectives  
- Core concepts  
- Skill‑building exercises  
- Scenario‑based practice  
- Competency checks  
- Instructor notes  
- Guided Links throughout

# 📘 **TRAINING MODULE — DOCUMENTING THREATS OR HARM**  
*A complete, instructor‑ready training module.*

---

# 🟦 **Module Overview**  
This module teaches personnel how to **accurately, objectively, and safely document threats or harmful incidents** so that protective teams can intervene effectively.

---

# 🟦 **1. Learning Objectives**  
By the end of this module, trainees will be able to:

- **Differentiate facts from interpretations**  
- **Capture verbatim statements accurately**  
- **Describe physical actions objectively**  
- **Record environmental context**  
- **Identify what evidence must be preserved**  
- **Use a standardized documentation format**  
- **Recognize when escalation is required**  

---

# 🟦 **2. Core Concepts**  
These are the foundational principles trainees must master.

### **A. Objectivity**  
- Document only what you **saw**, **heard**, or **collected**.  
- Avoid labels like “angry,” “crazy,” “violent.”  
- Use **observable behavior** instead:  
  - “Raised voice”  
  - “Stepped forward quickly”  
  - “Blocked exit path”

### **B. Verbatim Accuracy**  
- Threats must be captured **exactly as spoken**.  
- Use quotation marks.  
- Do not “clean up” grammar or profanity.

### **C. Chronological Clarity**  
- Events must be recorded in the order they occurred.  
- Use timestamps when possible.

### **D. Evidence Preservation**  
- Save original messages, photos, voicemails, notes.  
- Do not alter or forward originals.  
- Log where evidence is stored.

---

# 🟦 **3. Documentation Skills Training**  
This section teaches the practical skills.

### **Skill 1 — Capturing Verbatim Statements**  
Trainees practice writing down:

- Direct threats  
- Implied threats  
- Coercive statements  
- Demands  
- Ultimatums  

Example practice line:  
> “If you leave, I’ll come find you.”

### **Skill 2 — Describing Physical Actions**  
Trainees learn to describe:

- Movements  
- Gestures  
- Proximity  
- Contact  
- Object use  

Example:  
> “He stepped toward me rapidly and raised his hand.”

### **Skill 3 — Recording Environmental Context**  
Trainees practice noting:

- Lighting  
- Noise  
- Crowding  
- Exit availability  
- Objects present  

### **Skill 4 — Identifying and Logging Evidence**  
Trainees learn to:

- Photograph damage  
- Save messages  
- Capture screenshots  
- Log witness names  
- Preserve timestamps  

---

# 🟦 **4. Scenario‑Based Practice**  
Trainees work through realistic but non‑graphic scenarios.

---

## **Scenario 1 — Verbal Threat in a Workplace Hallway**  
Trainee must document:

- Exact words  
- Physical posture  
- Distance  
- Who else was present  
- Environmental details  

---

## **Scenario 2 — Intimidation in a Parking Lot**  
Trainee must capture:

- Blocking behavior  
- Tone of voice  
- Vehicle positioning  
- Lighting and visibility  
- Any witnesses  

---

## **Scenario 3 — Threatening Text Messages**  
Trainee must:

- Preserve original messages  
- Record timestamps  
- Avoid paraphrasing  
- Log screenshots as evidence  

---

# 🟦 **5. Competency Check**  
Trainees must demonstrate:

- Ability to write a complete incident report  
- Ability to distinguish fact vs. interpretation  
- Ability to capture verbatim statements  
- Ability to identify escalation triggers  
- Ability to preserve evidence correctly  

Instructors evaluate:

- Clarity  
- Accuracy  
- Completeness  
- Objectivity  
- Professionalism  

---

# 🟦 **6. Instructor Notes**  
Guidance for trainers:

- Reinforce that documentation is **not** about proving guilt — it’s about **capturing facts**.  
- Encourage trainees to slow down and avoid assumptions.  
- Emphasize that **timeliness** improves accuracy.  
- Remind trainees that **safety comes first** — documentation happens *after* the situation is safe.  
- Provide examples of strong vs. weak documentation.  

---

# 🟦 **7. Training Completion Criteria**  
A trainee is considered competent when they can:

- Produce a complete, objective incident report  
- Capture threats verbatim  
- Describe actions without interpretation  
- Log evidence correctly  
- Identify when escalation is required
